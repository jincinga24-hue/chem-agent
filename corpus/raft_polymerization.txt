RAFT Polymerization — Mechanism and Kinetics

RAFT (Reversible Addition-Fragmentation chain Transfer) is a reversible-deactivation
radical polymerization technique that gives polymers with controlled molecular weight,
narrow dispersity, and well-defined chain-end functionality. It was first reported by
the CSIRO group (Chiefari, Rizzardo, Thang, Moad et al.) in 1998.

Mechanism
The RAFT process introduces a thiocarbonylthio chain-transfer agent (CTA, also called
RAFT agent) of the general structure Z-C(=S)-S-R, where Z is the activating group and
R is a radical leaving group. Conventional radical initiation generates a propagating
radical Pn, which adds reversibly to the C=S bond of the CTA. The resulting intermediate
fragments to release a new radical (initially R, later Pm), which propagates a new chain.
At any moment most chains are dormant (capped by the CTA), and only a tiny fraction
carry the active radical, suppressing termination and giving living-like behaviour.

Key rate constants
- kp: propagation rate constant (L/mol/s). For styrene at 70 C, kp is approximately
  480 L/mol/s. For methyl methacrylate (MMA) at 70 C, kp is approximately 830 L/mol/s.
  IUPAC benchmarks (Beuermann and Buback, Prog Polym Sci 27 2002 191) give Arrhenius
  parameters for many monomers.
- kd: initiator decomposition rate constant (1/s). For AIBN at 70 C, kd is approximately
  3.2e-5 1/s, corresponding to a half life of about 6 hours.
- kt: termination rate constant (L/mol/s). Approximately 1e8 for most monomers, weakly
  temperature dependent.
- C_tr: chain transfer constant of the CTA, defined as k_tr / kp. Higher C_tr means
  faster CTA consumption and narrower dispersity. CDB (cumyl dithiobenzoate) has
  C_tr ~ 6000 for styrene; DDMAT and CPADB are typical CTAs for acrylates with
  C_tr ~ 30 - 600.

Pseudo-steady-state radical concentration
Assuming radicals reach steady state and termination is bimolecular, the radical
concentration is [P*] = sqrt(2 f kd [I] / kt), where f is the initiator efficiency
(typically 0.5 to 0.7 for AIBN). This is constant if [I] does not vary much.

First-order monomer consumption
With constant [P*], monomer consumption is first order: x(t) = 1 - exp(-kp [P*] t)
where x is the fractional conversion.

Mn and dispersity in ideal RAFT
For an ideal RAFT polymerization (full CTA consumption, no termination loss):
  DP_n(t) = x(t) [M]_0 / [CTA]_0
  Mn(t)  = DP_n(t) * MW_monomer + MW_CTA
The dispersity is given approximately by the Mueller equation:
  Dispersity ~ 1 + 1/DP_n + (1/C_tr) (2 - x) / x
This expression is valid for low-to-moderate conversion (x < 0.7); at higher
conversion termination becomes significant and dispersity broadens.

Choosing a CTA
The CTA Z and R groups must be matched to the monomer family:
- Dithiobenzoates (e.g. CDB) work well with more activated monomers (MMA, styrene).
- Trithiocarbonates (e.g. DDMAT, CPADB) work well with less activated monomers
  (acrylates, acrylamides, vinyl acetate).
A mismatched CTA gives slow initiation, high dispersity, or incomplete control.
