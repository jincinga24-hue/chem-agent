Antimicrobial Peptides (AMPs) — Design Principles

Antimicrobial peptides are short cationic and amphipathic peptides found in nearly
all forms of life as part of the innate immune defence. They typically contain 12 to
50 residues, carry net positive charge of +2 to +9 at physiological pH, and have a
significant fraction of hydrophobic residues. They kill bacteria primarily by
disrupting the bacterial membrane.

Key design parameters
1. Net charge. AMPs are selectively attracted to bacterial membranes because bacterial
   outer leaflets are rich in negatively charged lipids (phosphatidylglycerol, cardiolipin,
   lipopolysaccharide), whereas mammalian outer leaflets are predominantly zwitterionic
   (phosphatidylcholine). A net charge of +3 to +9 typically gives good selectivity
   without excessive haemolysis.

2. Hydrophobic moment. The hydrophobic moment uH (Eisenberg, Nature 299, 1982, 371)
   measures the amphipathicity of a peptide assumed to fold into an alpha-helix. It is
   defined as
     uH = (1/N) sqrt[ (sum H_i sin(delta i))^2 + (sum H_i cos(delta i))^2 ]
   where H_i is the hydrophobicity of residue i (Kyte-Doolittle or Eisenberg scale),
   delta is the residue angle (100 deg per residue for an alpha-helix, 180 deg for a
   beta-strand), and N is the number of residues. A high uH indicates that hydrophobic
   residues cluster on one face of the helix; this drives membrane partitioning.
   Active AMPs typically have alpha-helical uH > 0.4 on the normalised Eisenberg scale.

3. Mean hydrophobicity. The average hydrophobicity per residue determines how strongly
   the peptide partitions into membranes overall. Too hydrophobic and the peptide loses
   selectivity (binds mammalian membranes too); too hydrophilic and it fails to insert.

4. Secondary structure. Many AMPs are unstructured in solution and fold into
   alpha-helices upon membrane binding. Helix-forming residues (A, E, L, M with
   Chou-Fasman P_alpha > 1.1) favour formation; helix breakers (P, G with
   P_alpha < 0.6) disrupt it.

Mechanisms of membrane disruption
- Carpet model: peptides accumulate parallel to the membrane, eventually disintegrating
  it like a carpet.
- Barrel-stave: peptides insert perpendicular to the membrane and form a transmembrane
  pore lined by hydrophobic faces.
- Toroidal pore: peptides bend the bilayer into a curved pore stabilised by
  inward-facing peptide-lipid contacts.
The dominant mechanism depends on peptide concentration, membrane composition, and
the peptide's amphipathic geometry.

Examples
Melittin (GIGAVLKVLTTGLPALISWIKRKRQQ) from bee venom is a classic alpha-helical AMP.
Its 26 residues carry net charge +5 to +6 at pH 7.4, and its alpha-helical uH on the
Kyte-Doolittle scale is approximately 1.2 (or about 0.4 on the normalised Eisenberg
scale). Melittin is highly active but also haemolytic, illustrating the
selectivity-activity trade-off.

Magainin-2 (GIGKFLHSAKKFGKAFVGEIMNS) from frog skin is a less haemolytic alpha-helical
AMP, with charge approximately +4 and lower mean hydrophobicity than melittin.

Wimley AMP design heuristic (ACS Chem Biol 5, 2010, 905):
- Length: 8 to 30 residues
- Net charge: +3 to +9
- Hydrophobic moment uH (alpha helix): > 0.4 (normalised Eisenberg) or > 0.6 on
  Kyte-Doolittle
- Mean hydrophobicity: moderate, neither extreme.
