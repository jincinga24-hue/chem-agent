SNAPPs — Structurally Nanoengineered Antimicrobial Peptide Polymers

SNAPPs are a class of star-shaped cationic peptide polymers developed by Greg Qiao's
group at the University of Melbourne as a new approach to combat multidrug-resistant
(MDR) bacteria. The platform was first reported in 2016 (Lam, Wong, Boyer, Qiao et al.,
Nature Microbiology 1, 16162).

Architecture
A SNAPP is a hyperbranched star polymer with a polyamidoamine (PAMAM) or similar
multi-arm core, from which short cationic peptide arms emanate. The arms are typically
6 to 16 amino acids long, rich in lysine (K) and valine (V) or other hydrophobic
residues, giving each arm a high local positive charge density and amphipathic
character. Typical SNAPPs have 16 to 32 arms.

Synthesis
The peptide arms are grown from the multifunctional core by N-carboxyanhydride (NCA)
ring-opening polymerisation, which proceeds in a controlled manner without harsh
coupling reagents. The number of arms and arm length can be tuned by stoichiometry.

Mechanism of action
SNAPPs disrupt bacterial membranes via a multi-modal mechanism. The high local
density of positive charge enables strong electrostatic binding to negatively charged
bacterial outer leaflets (LPS in Gram-negative, teichoic acids in Gram-positive).
The amphipathic arms then insert into and depolarise the inner membrane, causing rapid
cell death. Because of their multi-modal action and the multiple arms required to drive
this mechanism, SNAPPs do not readily select for resistance.

Activity profile
SNAPPs show low minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) values (often 1 to 10 ug/mL)
against Gram-negative pathogens including E. coli, P. aeruginosa, A. baumannii, and
K. pneumoniae, including their multidrug-resistant strains. They show much lower
activity against mammalian red blood cells, giving therapeutic indices typically
greater than 50.

Lipidated SNAPP-Stars (2025)
A recent advance is the lipidated SNAPP-Star (Sun, Cheng, Lam, Qiao et al., ACS Applied
Materials and Interfaces, 2025). Adding a single fatty-acid chain (C6, C12, or C18) to
the core anchors the SNAPP to bacterial peptidoglycan, dramatically accelerating kill
kinetics. The optimised C6-SNAPP kills MRSA in under 1 minute, compared to over 16
hours for vancomycin, and selectively targets MRSA over commensal S. aureus in
co-culture. Lipidation does not increase mammalian cell toxicity, giving an improved
therapeutic index.

Computational and MD studies
Molecular dynamics simulations of SNAPPs interacting with bacterial and mammalian
bilayers (Jayawardena, Hung, Qiao 2025; J. Phys. Chem. B 5c02067 and 5c06308) have
been used to understand the molecular basis of selectivity. Lipidated SNAPPs show
preferential insertion into bacterial-mimicking bilayers (PG-rich) versus mammalian
RBC bilayers (PC-rich). The MD work complements the wet-lab synthesis pipeline.

Future directions
Computational design of SNAPP arm sequences using AMP descriptors (charge,
hydrophobic moment, helix propensity) and machine-learning models is an emerging
direction (Qiao et al., Prog. Polym. Sci. 2026, 102119). The combination of star
polymer architecture, peptide chemistry, and computational design positions SNAPPs as
a flexible platform against the antibiotic resistance crisis.
